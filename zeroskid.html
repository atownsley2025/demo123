ZeroSkid: Wet-Road Detection with AlexNet

ZeroSkid is an assistive driving system that uses computer vision and deep learning to detect wet pavement in real time. Wet roads cause thousands of preventable crashes every year. The goal of this project is to warn drivers before traction is lost.

Problem & Motivation

Wet pavement is a serious but often overlooked hazard. It contributes to a large number of crashes each year, along with major financial and emotional costs.

  • Current systems like ABS and traction control only react after a skid has started.
  • Drivers can’t always see thin layers of water, puddles, or slick surfaces, especially at night.
  • There is a clear need for proactive, real-time detection of wet-road conditions.

Crash statistics commonly report:
• Hundreds of thousands of wet-road crashes per year
• Over five hundred thousand injuries annually
• Thousands of deaths that could be reduced with better warnings

This project is motivated by the idea that AI and computer vision can help close the gap between what cars detect and what drivers can safely respond to in time.

ZeroSkid Device & Solution Overview

ZeroSkid proposes a small add-on device that uses a dash-mounted camera and an onboard deep learning model to classify the road ahead as dry or wet.

Core Ideas

  • Camera-based sensing of the lane ahead.
  • Real-time classification of road conditions (wet vs. dry).
  • Audio or visual alerts when wet pavement is detected.

Who Could Benefit

  • Everyday drivers in rain-heavy regions (for example, Florida).
  • Insurance partners trying to reduce crash risk.
  • Fleet and delivery operators who want fewer accidents and less downtime.

Technical Approach: Using AlexNet

ZeroSkid uses AlexNet, a deep convolutional neural network (CNN), to classify images of the road as wet or dry. AlexNet is well-suited to this task because its convolutional layers can learn visual patterns like texture, reflections, and edges on the pavement.

Architecture Summary

  • 5 convolutional layers for feature extraction.
  • 3 fully connected layers for final classification.
  • ReLU activations and max pooling to improve learning and reduce noise.
  • Softmax output layer for predicting wet vs. dry.

How It Helps

Early convolutional layers focus on edges and simple patterns; deeper layers pick up more complex textures and reflections. This layered processing helps the network distinguish between normal dry pavement and the shinier, more reflective look of wet roads.

Training Process & Data

The model is trained on labeled images of roads under wet and dry conditions. During training, AlexNet learns to connect visual patterns in the images with the correct label.

Training Setup

Input data: Wet & dry road images
Loss function: Cross-entropy loss
Optimizer: SGD or Adam
Learning rate: ~0.001
Batch size: 32
Epochs: 10–30 (depending on dataset size)

Data & Preprocessing

  • Images from different times of day and weather conditions.
  • Mix of highways, city streets, and parking lots.
  • All images resized to 224×224×3 RGB to match AlexNet.
  • Pixel values normalized for more stable training.
  • Data augmentation (brightness changes, rotations, flips) to improve generalization.

Takeaways

  • Wet roads are a major source of preventable crashes and injuries.
  • AlexNet can learn visual patterns that help detect wet pavement from camera images.
  • ZeroSkid shows how deep learning can be used for real-time, proactive driver assistance.
  • Future work could explore nighttime performance, black ice detection, and deployment on embedded hardware.

Overall, this project connects a real-world safety problem with a practical computer vision solution, using a well-known neural network architecture.
