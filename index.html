ZeroSkid: Driving Toward a Safer Future

ZeroSkid is an assistive driving device that uses computer vision and deep learning (AlexNet/CNNs) to detect wet-road conditions in real time. Wet pavement is a silent hazard that causes hundreds of thousands of preventable crashes every year. Our goal is to warn drivers before a skid happens.

Part 1 — Problem Overview

The Critical Problem: Wet Pavement as a Hidden Threat

Wet pavement is a quiet but serious hazard. It contributes to a staggering number of incidents each year, many of which could be prevented with earlier warnings.

  • Current vehicle safety systems like ABS and traction control only react after a skid has begun.
  • Drivers can’t always see thin layers of water, puddles, or slick surfaces—especially at night.
  • There is a gap between what the car senses and what the driver can safely react to in time.
$340B+ Annual crash-related costs in the U.S.
500k+ Injuries From wet-road incidents every year
5,700 Deaths Tragic losses that could be reduced with early intervention

Medical bills, insurance payouts, and lost productivity all add up. The human cost is even greater.

Part 1 — Solution & Motivation

ZeroSkid: A Proactive Wet-Road Safety System

ZeroSkid is designing a camera-based assistive driving device that leverages computer vision and deep learning to classify road conditions in real time. Instead of reacting after a skid, ZeroSkid provides proactive alerts.

Our Mission

  • Reduce crashes on wet pavement
  • Save lives and protect families
  • Cut economic costs for drivers, fleets, and insurers
  • Show how AI can deliver meaningful social benefits

Why Existing Systems Fall Short

  • ABS & Traction Control only activate once wheels have already started to slip.
  • No built-in system actively detects puddles or thin water layers on the road surface.
  • Human vision is limited in rain, glare, and low-light conditions.

This creates a clear opportunity: use AI and computer vision to sense and predict dangerous road conditions before the driver loses control.

Part 2 — Technical Solution

Technical Approach: Using AlexNet for Wet-Road Detection

At the core of ZeroSkid is AlexNet, a deep convolutional neural network (CNN) known for its strong image classification performance. AlexNet’s layered design makes it ideal for learning the subtle visual cues of wet pavement.

Its convolutional layers can detect textures, shine, and edges—exactly the kinds of patterns that distinguish dry asphalt from slick, reflective surfaces after rain.

Architecture Overview

  • 5 convolutional layers for feature extraction
  • 3 fully connected layers for classification
  • ReLU activations and max pooling to improve learning and reduce noise
  • Softmax output to predict wet vs. dry road classes

Layer-by-Layer Detection

  • Convolutional Layers: Extract edges, patterns, reflections, and textures from road images.
  • ReLU Activation: Adds non-linearity so the model can learn more complex relationships.
  • Pooling Layers: Downsample feature maps, making the model more efficient and robust to small variations.
  • Fully Connected Layers: Combine all learned features to make the final wet/dry decision.

Together, these layers let ZeroSkid reliably classify whether a lane ahead is safe or dangerously slick.

Part 2 — Training & Data

ZeroSkid Training: Data, Process, and Preprocessing

Training Process

Input data: Wet & dry road images
Loss function: Cross-entropy loss
Optimizer: SGD or Adam
Learning rate: ~0.001
Batch size: 32
Epochs: 10–30 (depending on dataset size)

The model learns by iteratively minimizing cross-entropy loss, adjusting its weights to better match the true labels (wet vs. dry) over many training epochs.

Data Requirements & Preprocessing

  • Images of roads under light rain, heavy rain, puddles, and dry conditions.
  • Day and night scenarios, highways, city streets, and parking lots.
  • Input resized to 224×224×3 RGB to match AlexNet’s expected input size.
  • Pixel values normalized (0–1 or -1 to 1) for stable training.
  • Data augmentation including brightness changes, rotations, and horizontal flips.

These steps help the model generalize to real-world conditions and avoid overfitting to a narrow set of images.
