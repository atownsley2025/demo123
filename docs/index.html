Problem & Motivation

Why wet roads need better warnings.

Hydroplaning can happen in a split second. By the time ABS and traction control react, the car might already be sliding. This project explores how computer vision could give a heads-up before that moment.

The safety gap

Most modern vehicles are designed to react after the driver has already started to lose traction. On wet pavement, especially at higher speeds, that reaction can come too late. There is a gap between “the road is getting risky” and “the car is already out of control.”

  • Wet pavement is responsible for many weather-related crashes and injuries each year.
  • Drivers don’t always realize how much grip they’ve lost until it’s too late.
  • Existing systems rarely tell you that the surface itself is the problem.

ZeroSkid asks a simple question: what if the car’s camera could recognize wet pavement early and warn the driver before they reach that dangerous point?

Goals for this project

This is a prototype, not a production system. The goal was to build, train, and analyze a model that:

  • Classifies images of pavement as either wet or dry.
  • Uses AlexNet as a starting point and explores how far tuning can go.
  • Connects the model’s behavior back to real-world driving and safety questions.

Technical Approach

How I used AlexNet to see wet pavement.

Under the hood, ZeroSkid uses a convolutional neural network (AlexNet) that was originally trained on ImageNet. I fine-tuned it on a wet/dry road dataset and ran several experiments to understand how training choices affected performance.

Dataset & setup

I built a two-class dataset focused on clear views of road surfaces labeled as wet or dry. All images were resized and normalized to match AlexNet’s expected input format and split into training, validation, and test sets.

  • Two labels: wet pavement and dry pavement.
  • Images preprocessed to AlexNet’s input size and normalized.
  • Training, validation, and test splits used to keep evaluation fair.

Everything was implemented in PyTorch and run in Google Colab, which made it easy to track experiments and export plots like the ones shown on this site.

Model & training summary

I started from a pre-trained AlexNet and adapted its final layers for my two classes. I trained the classifier with cross-entropy loss and experimented with different hyperparameters to see what helped the model learn the most.

  • Base model: pre-trained AlexNet (convolutional + pooling layers).
  • Final layer swapped for a 2-class output (wet vs. dry).
  • Trained with cross-entropy loss and an optimizer like SGD/Adam.

From there, I ran multiple experiments that changed the learning rate and tracked accuracy and loss over time.

Results

What I learned from the experiments.

Instead of training one model and stopping, I treated this as a small research project. Comparing the baseline (Experiment 1) to the tuned run (Experiment 3) helped me understand how training choices show up in the plots and final behavior.

High-level findings

The training curves reveal that the “default” setup in Experiment 1 learned something, but it wasn’t the most stable. Experiment 3, with a lower learning rate, showed smoother accuracy growth and a clearer separation between training and validation performance.

  • Experiment 1 gave me a baseline and showed how quickly the model could overfit without careful tuning.
  • Experiment 3 improved validation behavior and produced cleaner curves across epochs.
  • The confusion matrix for the best model suggests it can correctly identify many wet and dry cases, but still has room to improve.

Even without exact percentages, the plots and confusion matrix make it clear that training choices like the learning rate have a big impact on how reliable the model is.

Takeaways & Future Work

What this project taught me.

ZeroSkid is just one class project, but it shows how computer vision and careful experiments can connect to real-world safety questions about driving on wet roads.

Key takeaways

  • Small changes can matter a lot. Adjusting something as simple as the learning rate made a clear difference in my accuracy and loss curves.
  • Plots tell the story. Looking at how accuracy and loss changed over time helped me understand what the model was doing, not just what the final number was.
  • Evaluation isn’t just one score. The confusion matrix showed where the model did well and where it still struggled.
  • Safety context stays important. Even a “good” model has to be tested in real conditions before it could be trusted in a car.

Future directions

  • Collect more varied data from different roads, times of day, and weather conditions.
  • Test other architectures and lighter models that could run on in-car hardware.
  • Move from single images to short video clips so the system can see how the road changes over time.
  • Design how a real car interface might warn drivers about dangerous wet pavement without distracting them.

Overall, this project was a hands-on way to connect deep learning, experiment design, and road safety into one topic: keeping drivers from reaching the moment where they lose traction in the first place.
